# Customer Discovery — In Their Own Words

> This document captures what Lake Stevens Public Works told us during the discovery session, organized by who said it and what they need. Every statement traces to the recorded session transcript.

---

## The People

**Eric** — Capital Projects Manager, ~14 active projects
**Shannon** — Project Coordinator, ~7 active projects (primarily design phase)
**Lori** — Administrative Coordinator, manages document logistics and SharePoint

## How We Work Today

### Shannon — Invoice Processing (the daily workflow)

When a consultant sends us an invoice, our Finance department gets it via email. They put it in a designated SharePoint folder — basically a folder that says "Shannon needs to find it." I get an email notification that something's there.

I open the invoice PDF, review it, and then I navigate to my project tracking spreadsheet. I have hotlinks to my spreadsheets in the capital project folder. I log the invoice data by task — project management, surveying, environmental, that kind of thing. Then I can see: how much did we spend? How much is remaining? What's the percent complete?

Then I do my gut check. I look at this and go, "Okay, this is saying 13% complete. Where are we at on this project?" If it sounds right, great. If they've spent almost all their budget and we don't even have our 30% design yet — well, that's a problem.

Once it looks good, I sign the invoice through Adobe Sign, and then I move it to the "approved" folder.

> *"90% of the time, this is the standard process. Variations occur only in the spreadsheet detail level."*

### Shannon — Grant Reimbursement

I track which grant each invoice is coded to in my spreadsheet. When it's time for a reimbursement, I go look at the invoice numbers, then go to our SharePoint invoice folders and search for each one. I cross-reference them, pull them together, and that becomes my reimbursement package.

> *"I have invoice numbers here. And when I go to code this, I will put which grant it's for. When I go to do reimbursement, I'll come down and see these three invoices I sent in our last reimbursement."*

### Eric — My Spreadsheet (the most comprehensive one)

Let me show you my Main Street Improvements project. The overview has the project name, the CFP number, our project number. Then I have budget sources with Springbrook budget codes — like 301-000-333-20-20-50 — and multiple funding sources. FHWA Grant, TIZ 1, R2 Main.

Each contract type gets its own tab. Design, ROW, CM Services, Construction. The links in the spreadsheet go directly to the signed contracts on SharePoint — that's the actual signed contract, supplement one, supplement two, all linked directly.

For invoices, I log the invoice number, the amounts, and track the running total. Our contract remaining updates automatically. Right-of-way is tracked separately by parcel number — that's a different animal than regular invoicing.

> *"The links lead to the contract... that's the actual signed contract. We had a supplementary contract here. Supplement one. Supplement two. All the signed. Link directly to the signed. Which lives in SharePoint."*

### Eric — The Finance Team's Parallel World

There's a whole separate tracking process on the Finance side. They track post-payment data through Springbrook — our financial software. Every capital project has budget codes tied to Springbrook account numbers. They have their own master tracker.

The problem is I'm duplicating their work. I track the pre-payment side — what we've been invoiced for. They track the post-payment side — what checks have actually cleared. There's always a gap between what I see and what they see.

> *"Our version is the debit card view. Their version is the check-cleared, bank account view. There's always a delta because we've got invoices I've already removed the money for, but they haven't necessarily paid yet."*

> *"I don't really care what we paid... I'm more worried about what we're spending out of."*

## Our Pain Points

### 1. SharePoint Metadata — Eric's #1 Frustration

> *"The biggest pain point now with SharePoint is that we have to manually put all the metadata in."*

Every time a document goes into SharePoint, someone has to manually tag it with project name, document type, dates, contractor — all by hand. It's tedious and error-prone.

### 2. No Standards — We Each Do It Differently

We have at least three different spreadsheet formats across just a few project managers. Shannon's spreadsheets vary by project — some are detailed, some are high-level. Eric's are comprehensive but follow his own structure. We've always known we should standardize, but we just haven't had the time.

> *Eric: "Our biggest need right now is just the organization and creating standards for what everyone's doing. Making sure that everyone does it the same way."*

> *Shannon: "I think we could easily standardize whatever we need to. Just our challenge is having the time to go and standardize it."*

> *Eric: "Even if we have three different standards, at least then it's only three."*

### 3. Manual Invoice Logging Is Time-Consuming

Every single invoice requires manual spreadsheet entry. When you have 7 capital projects — or Eric's 14 — and multiple invoices coming in, it just bogs everything down. Open the PDF, switch to the spreadsheet, find the right tab, enter each task line, verify the total. Repeat.

> *Shannon: "It's a pretty quick process, but you can imagine I have like seven capital projects, Eric has 14. When you're getting a lot of invoices, it just kind of gets bogged down."*

### 4. Everything Is in Different Places

Contracts end up in different spots. Sometimes they're in the project folder, sometimes they're in a general contracts folder, sometimes they're duplicated in both. Invoices are in one SharePoint section, budgets in another, grant documents somewhere else. There's no single place to see everything about a project.

> *Eric: "Sometimes contracts are in the project folder. Sometimes contracts are in our general contract folder. Usually it's more like a duplicate."*

### 5. No Dashboard — No "Bird's Eye View"

We can't see all of our projects at once with budget health, invoice status, and alerts in one view. Eric's Capital Gantt spreadsheet is the closest thing we have, but it requires manual updates.

> *Eric: "What I'm envisioning is we have this one area where you can see all this information about a project. You can see the invoices, how much has been spent, what's the contract total. And then you can get to all the different components from that one view."*

## What We Want

### Keep Our Existing Systems — Just Make Them Work Together

We're not looking to replace SharePoint. We're not looking to replace Springbrook. We're not looking to replace Adobe Sign. We just want something that pulls all of this together so we're not doing all the manual work in between.

> *Daniel: "Integrate with the systems we have and make your life better."*
> *Eric: "That sounds better."*

### One View Per Project — With Everything Connected

I want to open a project and see: the budget, the contracts (with links to signed documents), the invoices, the grant coding, the right-of-way parcels, the funding sources — all in one place. But that view is pulling from all our different data sources, not making me re-enter anything.

### Automated Gut-Checks

The manual process of comparing "percent spent" vs "percent complete" and going "does that sound right?" — automate that. Show me red/yellow/green immediately. Don't make me do the mental math.

### Import What We Already Have

We're not starting over. We've got years of spreadsheet data. We want to upload what we have and be running. No re-entry.

### Export for Safety

During the transition, we still want spreadsheets on SharePoint. That's our safety net. Over time, maybe we won't need them. But for now, give us the option.

### Standardize Without the Work

We know we need to standardize. We've just never had the time. If the system can take our different formats and normalize them into one standard — that solves our problem without us having to do anything.

### Project Templates (Future)

Eric's already building milestone checklists in Microsoft Planner. When we start a new capital project, certain things always need to happen. Having templates that automatically set up the project structure would save a lot of setup time.

> *Eric: "There's this parallel process I've been doing in Planner already. I know every project has certain milestones. It acts like a checklist. Having the template there is good."*

## Key Identifiers We Use

| What We Call It | What It Means | Where It Lives |
|----------------|---------------|----------------|
| CFP # | Capital Facilities Plan number | Adopted by council, standardized across departments |
| Project Number | Our internal project ID (e.g., RD-101) | Assigned per project, standardized |
| Budget Code | Springbrook account number | Finance/ERP system |
| Invoice # | Consultant's invoice number | On the invoice PDF itself — Shannon's primary lookup key |

---

## The Ground Rules We All Agreed On

1. **IPC is a coordination layer** — it doesn't replace existing systems
2. **SharePoint stays** — for document storage and metadata
3. **Springbrook stays** — for financial tracking (read-only from IPC)
4. **Adobe Sign stays** — for signatures
5. **IPC never writes to Finance systems** — display-only for budget codes
6. **Export is always available** — no lock-in
7. **The app should be the tracker** — not generate spreadsheets, but BE the standardized view
